Fireballs, what are they?

Fireballs are abnormally bright meteors/ shooting stars that are dramatic enough to be seen across wide areas and reach a magnitude of -3 or brighter at its highest point.


The Dataset

This dataset contains fireball sighting events and data from CNEOS (Center for Near Earth Object Studies) reported by US Government sensors.
Data dates as far as back as 1988 and currently contains more than 900 sightings. The dataset is not inclusive of all fireball occurrences.


Data Components

Peak Brightness Date/Time (UT) - Date/time in UT (Universal Time) of event's peak brightness.
Latitude (deg.) - Geodetic latitude in degrees north (N) or south (S) of the equator for event.
Longitude (deg.) - Geodetic longitude in degrees east (E) or west (W) of the prime meridian for event.
Altitude (km) - Altitude in kilometers (km) above the reference geoid for this event.
Velocity (km/s) - Magnitude of the meteor's pre-impact velocity in kilometers per second (km/s).
Velocity (km/s) vx - Pre-entry estimated velocity (Earth centered X component).
Velocity (km/s) vy - Pre-entry estimated velocity (Earth centered Y component).
Velocity (km/s) vz - Pre-entry estimated velocity (Earth centered Z component).
Total Radiated Energy (J) - The approximate total radiated energy in the atmosphere in Joules.
Calculated Total Impact Energy (kt) - Impact energy of the event in kilotons of TNT (kt).
